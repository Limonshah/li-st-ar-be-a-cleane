## Cleaning Railroad Ties: A Step-by-Step Guide

Have you noticed your beautiful railroad ties looking a little worse for wear? Over time, these landscape features can accumulate dirt, algae, and mildew, diminishing their aesthetic appeal. Cleaning railroad ties isn’t just about looks; it’s about preserving the wood and extending its life. This article will walk you through everything you need to know to effectively clean your railroad ties and keep them looking their best. We’ll cover preparation, cleaning methods, and protective measures to ensure lasting results.

**Takeaway:**

*   Prepare the area by removing debris and protecting surrounding plants.
*   Choose the right cleaning method: scrubbing, pressure washing, or a chemical solution.
*   Apply a wood preservative to protect the ties after cleaning.
*   Regular maintenance will prevent buildup and keep your ties looking great.

**What's the quickest way to clean railroad ties?**

A quick clean involves sweeping away loose debris, scrubbing with a stiff brush and a mild detergent solution, and rinsing thoroughly with water. For more stubborn grime, a low-pressure wash can be effective, but always test a small area first.

## 1. Understanding Railroad Tie Composition & Why Cleaning Matters

Railroad ties, traditionally made from creosote-treated wood, are incredibly durable but still susceptible to weathering. Creosote is a powerful preservative, but it can also leach out over time, making the wood more vulnerable to the elements. Cleaning removes surface contaminants that accelerate deterioration, like algae and mildew, and helps maintain the protective qualities of the remaining creosote. Understanding the material you’re working with is the first step to effective cleaning. 

Regular cleaning also prevents a slippery surface, especially important if the ties are used as steps or walkways.  Ignoring this maintenance can lead to costly replacements down the line. ## 2. Preparing to Clean Your Railroad Ties

Before you start cleaning, proper preparation is key. This protects your landscaping and ensures a more efficient cleaning process. Begin by removing any plants, furniture, or other objects from the immediate area around the ties. Cover any nearby plants with plastic sheeting to shield them from cleaning solutions or pressure washing spray. 

Next, sweep away loose dirt, leaves, and debris with a broom or leaf blower. This initial step makes the actual cleaning much easier.  Finally, inspect the ties for any loose or damaged wood that needs repair before you begin. ## 3. Choosing Your Cleaning Method: Scrubbing, Pressure Washing, or Chemicals

There are several ways to clean railroad ties, each with its pros and cons. The best method depends on the level of dirt and grime, and your personal preference. 

*   **Scrubbing:** This is the gentlest method, ideal for lightly soiled ties. Use a stiff-bristled brush, warm water, and a mild detergent.
*   **Pressure Washing:** Effective for removing stubborn dirt and algae, but use caution! High pressure can damage the wood. Always start with a low setting and test a small area first.
*   **Chemical Solutions:** Wood cleaners specifically designed for outdoor use can dissolve tough stains and mildew. Follow the manufacturer's instructions carefully and always wear protective gear. ## 4. Step-by-Step Cleaning Instructions

Let's get into the details of each cleaning method.

**Scrubbing:** Mix warm water with a mild detergent in a bucket. Dip the brush into the solution and scrub the ties in the direction of the wood grain. Rinse thoroughly with clean water.

**Pressure Washing:** Start with a low-pressure setting and a wide-angle nozzle. Hold the nozzle at least 12 inches away from the tie and move it in a sweeping motion. Avoid concentrating the spray on one spot for too long.

**Chemical Cleaning:** Apply the wood cleaner according to the manufacturer's instructions. Let it sit for the recommended time, then scrub with a brush and rinse thoroughly.  Remember to always wear gloves and eye protection when using chemical cleaners. ## 5. Dealing with Stubborn Stains and Mildew

Sometimes, simple cleaning isn't enough. Stubborn stains like rust or mildew require extra attention. For rust stains, try a rust remover specifically designed for wood. Apply it according to the instructions and rinse thoroughly. 

Mildew can be tackled with a solution of bleach and water (1 part bleach to 3 parts water). Apply the solution to the affected area, let it sit for 10-15 minutes, and then scrub with a brush and rinse. *Always* test this solution in an inconspicuous area first, as bleach can discolor the wood. Cleaning is only half the battle. Protecting your railroad ties after cleaning will help them stay looking their best for years to come. Applying a wood preservative or sealant will help repel water, prevent mildew growth, and protect the wood from UV damage. 

Choose a preservative specifically designed for outdoor use and follow the manufacturer's instructions carefully. Reapply the preservative every 1-2 years, or as needed. ## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Can I use a power washer on railroad ties?**
A: Yes, but with caution. Use a low-pressure setting and a wide-angle nozzle to avoid damaging the wood. Always test a small area first.

**Q: Is creosote harmful?**
A: Creosote is a preservative, but it can be harmful if ingested or if you have prolonged skin contact. Wear gloves and eye protection when cleaning railroad ties.

**Q: How often should I clean my railroad ties?**
A:  Generally, cleaning once a year is sufficient. However, if you live in a humid climate or the ties are exposed to a lot of dirt and debris, you may need to clean them more frequently.

**Q: What kind of wood preservative should I use?**
A: Choose a wood preservative specifically designed for outdoor use and compatible with creosote-treated wood. Look for products that offer UV protection and mildew resistance.

**Q: Can I paint or stain railroad ties?**
A: While possible, it's generally not recommended. Paint or stain can trap moisture and accelerate deterioration. If you do choose to paint or stain, use a breathable product specifically designed for outdoor wood.
