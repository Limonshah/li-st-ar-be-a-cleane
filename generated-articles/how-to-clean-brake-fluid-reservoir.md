## Keeping Your Brakes Sharp: How To Clean a Brake Fluid Reservoir

Have you ever wondered what’s going on under the hood of your car? One often-overlooked component is the brake fluid reservoir. This small container plays a vital role in your vehicle’s safety, and keeping it clean is crucial for optimal brake performance. A dirty brake fluid reservoir can lead to contamination, corrosion, and ultimately, brake failure. This guide will walk you through how to clean your brake fluid reservoir, ensuring your brakes remain reliable and responsive. We’ll cover everything from safety precautions to the step-by-step cleaning process.

**Takeaway:**

*   Regularly inspect your brake fluid reservoir for debris.
*   Use the correct type of brake fluid for your vehicle.
*   Clean the reservoir carefully to avoid contamination.
*   Proper maintenance extends the life of your braking system.

**What does it mean to clean a brake fluid reservoir?**

Cleaning a brake fluid reservoir involves removing sediment, rust, and other contaminants that accumulate over time. This ensures the brake fluid remains clean and functions correctly, maintaining optimal braking performance and preventing damage to the brake system.

## Why Cleaning Your Brake Fluid Reservoir Matters

Your brake fluid reservoir isn’t just a container; it’s a critical part of your braking system. Over time, moisture, dirt, and rubber particles from the brake lines can accumulate inside the reservoir. This contamination can degrade the brake fluid, reducing its effectiveness and potentially causing corrosion within the brake system.  Regular cleaning helps prevent these issues, ensuring consistent braking power and extending the life of your brake components. Ignoring this maintenance can lead to costly repairs down the road.

## Safety First: Precautions Before You Start

Brake fluid is corrosive and can damage paint, so safety is paramount. Before you begin, gather your supplies: new brake fluid (compatible with your vehicle – check your owner’s manual!), clean cloths or paper towels, a syringe or turkey baster, safety glasses, and gloves.  Always work in a well-ventilated area.  Protect your car’s paint by covering surrounding areas with plastic sheeting or drop cloths.  Remember, brake fluid is hazardous, so avoid skin contact and dispose of used fluid properly – never pour it down the drain. If you spill any, immediately clean it up with plenty of water.

## Tools and Materials You’ll Need

Having the right tools makes the job much easier. Here’s a checklist of what you’ll need to clean your brake fluid reservoir effectively:

*   **New Brake Fluid:**  Use the correct DOT specification for your vehicle.
*   **Clean Cloths/Paper Towels:** For wiping and cleaning.
*   **Syringe or Turkey Baster:** To remove old brake fluid.
*   **Safety Glasses:** To protect your eyes from splashes.
*   **Gloves:** To protect your skin from corrosion.
*   **Plastic Sheeting/Drop Cloths:** To protect your car’s paint.
*   **Funnel:** For adding new brake fluid.
*   **Brake Cleaner (Optional):** For stubborn residue.

## Step-by-Step Guide to Cleaning the Reservoir

Now, let's get to the cleaning process. First, locate the brake fluid reservoir under the hood – it’s usually a translucent plastic container near the master cylinder.  Carefully remove the reservoir cap. Using your syringe or turkey baster, remove as much of the old brake fluid as possible.  Dispose of the old fluid responsibly. Next, inspect the inside of the reservoir for sediment or debris. If there’s stubborn residue, you can carefully use a small amount of brake cleaner, but ensure it’s completely removed before adding new fluid. Finally, refill the reservoir with fresh brake fluid to the “MAX” line, and securely replace the cap.  

## Inspecting and Flushing the Brake Lines (When to Go Further)

Cleaning the reservoir is a good start, but sometimes the entire brake system needs attention. If you notice significant contamination in the reservoir, or if your brake fluid is dark and murky, it might be time to flush the entire brake line system.  Flushing involves replacing all the old brake fluid with fresh fluid, removing any contaminants throughout the system. This is a more involved process, often best left to a professional mechanic. ## Maintaining a Clean Brake Fluid Reservoir: Prevention is Key

Once you’ve cleaned your brake fluid reservoir, maintaining its cleanliness is relatively simple. Regularly check the fluid level and color.  Dark or cloudy fluid indicates contamination and the need for a flush.  Avoid overfilling the reservoir, as this can cause issues with the braking system.  Consider having your brake fluid flushed professionally every two to three years, or as recommended by your vehicle’s manufacturer.  Also, remember to keep the reservoir cap tightly sealed to prevent moisture and debris from entering. ## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

**Q: How often should I clean my brake fluid reservoir?**

A:  It’s recommended to inspect your brake fluid reservoir at least once a year, and clean it if you notice any sediment or discoloration.  More frequent checks may be necessary if you drive in harsh conditions.

**Q: What type of brake fluid should I use?**

A: Always use the brake fluid specified in your vehicle’s owner’s manual. Using the wrong type can damage your braking system.

**Q: Can I mix different types of brake fluid?**

A: No, mixing different types of brake fluid can cause compatibility issues and reduce braking performance.

**Q: What should I do with the old brake fluid?**

A: Old brake fluid is considered hazardous waste and should be disposed of properly at a designated recycling center or auto parts store.

**Q: Is it difficult to flush the brake lines myself?**

A: Flushing brake lines is a more complex procedure than cleaning the reservoir and may require specialized tools and knowledge. If you’re not comfortable with automotive repair, it’s best to have a professional mechanic do it.
